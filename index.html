Market Overview

Tariff-induced market uncertainty a bad sign for IPOs

[Chart: Trailing 12-month VC-Backed IPO Index price/sales ratio versus VIX Index]

Volatility and IPOs were Q2's defining features. The quarter opened with a jolt from Liberation Day tariff announcements, plummeting the S&P 500 by over 10% in a few days and prompting many startups that had already filed to hit the brakes on going public. In a partial reversal, the Trump administration soon issued a 90-day pause on tariffs, which helped steady public equities.

In June, consumer sentiment recovered for the first time since December 2024 as consumers began adjusting to the tariff shock, even though they remain wary.

Key Insight: Despite the rocky start, venture exit activity began showing signs of life by the end of Q2 with a handful of high-profile IPOs and billion-dollar M&A deals.

Secondaries have grown but remain a niche play

[Chart: Estimated unicorn market size by strategy - Primary unicorns: $3,206.8B | Secondary market: $60.0B]

Our current estimate of the US VC direct secondary market ranges from $48.1 billion to $71.5 billion, with a midpoint of $60 billion. However, the market size is modest compared with VC's liquidity needs. $60 billion makes up less than 2% of total unicorn valuations and is comparable to Q2's primary exit value of $67.7 billion.

Trading volume continues to be concentrated among a narrow slice of elite startups that have the highest primary and secondary investor demand and, consequently, strong pricing confidence. For example, Hiive reported that just 20 startups accounted for 83.2% of trading volume in Q1, with the top five alone representing over half.

Key Trend: VC-specific secondary dry powder has more than doubled since 2022, and tender offers are becoming more common, providing companies price certainty and low cap table churn while still offering early employees and investors a path to partial liquidity.

Secondaries dry powder has more than doubled since 2022

[Chart: VC secondaries dry powder - Total: $9.8B with breakdown by vintage from Pre-2018 to 2024]

Multiple signs point to the continued expansion of secondaries as a structural pillar of the venture ecosystem, especially in a landscape where IPOs and M&A remain selective and lumpy. VC-specific secondary dry powder has more than doubled since 2022.

Secondaries are no longer a last resort, but rather a strategic tool. The market has matured significantly, with dedicated funds and platforms emerging to facilitate these transactions.

[Chart: Later stage has the largest gap between VC demand and supply - Demand/supply ratio by quarter showing Early-Stage, Late-Stage, and Venture-Growth-Stage trends]

The demand-supply ratio varies significantly by stage, with later-stage companies showing the most significant imbalance. This creates opportunities for secondary transactions as a pressure relief valve.

NVCA Policy Highlights

In a dramatic late-night session, the US House of Representatives narrowly approved the Senate's version of the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" by a 218–214 vote, sending the legislation to President Donald Trump just ahead of the self-imposed July 4th deadline.

Key Policy Wins for NVCA

  • Carried interest: No changes made. Existing tax treatment remains.
  • Qualified small business stock expansion: Introduces a tiered capital gains exclusion on stock purchased post-enactment: 50% exclusion after three years, 75% after four years, and 100% after five years.
  • R&D expensing: Allows immediate, permanent expensing of R&D expenditures. Small businesses with up to $31 million in gross receipts may apply this benefit retroactively to tax year 2022.

Areas of Concern

University endowment tax overhaul: The bill replaces the current excise tax on net investment income with a three-tiered system based on per-student endowment levels. Universities with 3,000 or fewer tuition-paying students are exempt.

Clean energy tax credits: The bill makes significant changes to energy tax credits, especially those from the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA).

Dealmaking

Q2 deal count remains roughly flat, while deal value dropped by 25%

[Chart: VC deal activity by quarter showing deal value and count from 2020-2025]

In Q2 2025, VC firms deployed $69.9 billion across an estimated 4,001 deals, representing a sharp 24.8% QoQ decline in deal value. This drop was due primarily to the lack of a $40 billion deal in Q2. OpenAI's deal from Q1 would alone represent roughly 57% of Q2's aggregate.

Fueled by select outsized Q1 deals and persistent AI momentum, venture-growth-stage deal activity surged in H1 2025, with $83.9 billion deployed across an estimated 499 deals. On an annualized basis, this implies $167.8 billion in growth-stage venture capital deployment, far surpassing the 2021 peak of $91.6 billion.

AI continues to dominate the upper end of the deal spectrum. In Q2, five AI deals topped $1 billion, including Scale AI, Safe Superintelligence, Thinking Machine Labs, Anduril, and Grammarly. The sector represented 64.1% and 35.6% of H1 2025 deal value and count, respectively.

The IPO markets appear to be back on the road to recovery

Although overall IPO activity is tracking behind optimistic expectations coming into the year, this is mainly due to macro-and-policy-uncertainty-induced market volatility, which has since retreated. David Bauer, co-head of Equity Capital Markets, notes that more recently there have been numerous green shoots, which is expected to provide a much-improved backdrop for tech issuance through the rest of the year.

The recent market rally post-Liberation Day, robust investor liquidity, and consistent IPO performance have created the most attractive issuance conditions in several years.

Key Observations:

  • Increased mutual fund participation with books multiple times oversubscribed
  • Greater interest for sub-$5 billion market cap issuers
  • IPO discounts in the 30% area reflecting current market realities
  • Some issuers bringing forward 2026 IPO plans to Q4 2025

Tech sector M&A also on the upswing

According to Vineet Seth, head of technology M&A, similar to the IPO market, M&A activity within the technology sector is also accelerating, particularly in software. Venture capital, growth equity, and private equity firms have held portfolio assets longer than usual, supporting a solid pipeline of potential M&A as these private businesses are brought to market over the coming months and quarters.

After a turbulent few weeks post-Liberation Day, previously prevailing themes in venture are back

While trade and tariff policy uncertainty remains an overhang, the stronger-than-expected first quarter earnings season, recovery in equity markets, and resilience of AI have gone a long way to restore sentiment across the venture ecosystem.

The market correction and volatility in April was significant but relatively orderly and short-lived. As the waters have calmed, equity issuance volumes have begun to improve and M&A activity involving startups has resumed the positive pace set earlier in the year.

Deal sizes continue to rise

Healthcare deal activity in H1 2025 shows a more cautious investment environment compared to the previous year, though deal sizes have increased significantly.
